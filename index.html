About Us

Cultivating Patience, Perseverance & Community

The Rhode Island Kung Fu Club is a 501(c)(3) non-profit organization that has taught the martial and cultural arts of kung fu and Chinese lion dance to Providence-area youth since 2007.

We operate through volunteer efforts and welcome members of all cultural backgrounds. Our mission is to utilize martial arts training to assist young people in their maturation and growth.

The effort and commitment required to develop kung fu and lion dance skills cultivates patience and perseverance—cornerstone ideals that will serve our members well throughout their lives.

What We Teach

Our Programs

Kung Fu Training

Jow Ga Style

Our kung fu curriculum is rooted in the Jow Ga system, a traditional Southern Chinese martial art known for its powerful stances, dynamic kicks, and fluid hand techniques.

  • Traditional forms and techniques
  • Self-defense applications
  • Physical conditioning
  • Weapons training (advanced)
  • Competition preparation

RIKFC members have participated in martial arts competitions throughout New England and the East Coast.

Chinese Lion Dance

Southern Lion Style

Chinese lion dance (舞獅) is a festive, cultural performance combining the movement and spirit of kung fu with drumming and percussion. It symbolizes fostering positivity and warding away negative energy.

  • Lion head and tail coordination
  • Traditional drumming patterns
  • Cymbal and gong techniques
  • Cai Qing (採青) - "Plucking the Greens"
  • Performance choreography
The Lucky Lettuce Tradition

During performances, our lions perform Cai Qing—plucking lettuce hung above doorways. The word for lettuce (菜/Cai) sounds like "wealth" in Chinese. By scattering the greens, the lion spreads prosperity and good fortune to all present.

Youth Development Focus

We collaborate with local schools and community centers through our outreach program, providing access to kung fu instruction for Providence-area youth. Our training instills values that extend far beyond the practice floor.
